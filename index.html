Cardiovascular Adaptation
Training causes a cascade of structural and functional cardiac adaptations collectively called "athlete's heart." Left ventricular hypertrophy (eccentric) increases stroke volume from ~70ml at rest (untrained) to 120–200ml (elite endurance). Plasma volume expansion (+15–20% in first 2 weeks) is the FASTEST adaptation — begins in session 1. VO₂Max improvements lag by 4–8 weeks but plasma expansion means HR drops at the same workload almost immediately. This is why your walking HR of 129 bpm will drop to ~100 bpm by Week 4–5 with consistent training.
Scharhag et al. 2013 — athlete's heart morphology; Montain & Coyle 1992 — plasma volume expansion
Mitochondrial Biogenesis
Zone 2 training activates PGC-1α, the "master regulator" of mitochondrial biogenesis. Each Zone 2 session signals the synthesis of new mitochondria in slow-twitch type I muscle fibres. More mitochondria = more sites for oxidative phosphorylation = higher fat oxidation capacity = lower glycogen burn rate at race pace. Increases mitochondrial density by 30–40% in 8 weeks (Holloszy 1967 — the foundational paper). This is WHY every Zone 2 run counts, even if it feels too easy.
Holloszy 1967; Hoppeler et al. 1985; Hood et al. 2011
Lactate Dynamics
Lactate is not waste — it is a metabolite, fuel, and signaling molecule (Brooks 2018). At Zone 2 intensities, lactate production and clearance are balanced. The lactate threshold (LT) is the intensity at which production outpaces clearance. LT1 (aerobic threshold): ~2 mmol/L — conversation pace. LT2 (anaerobic threshold/MLSS): ~4 mmol/L — race pace for HM. Training at LT2 (threshold runs) shifts both thresholds rightward — you can sustain the same lactate concentration at higher speeds. For VDOT 47: LT2 pace ≈ 5:01/km.
Brooks 2018; Faude et al. 2009 — MLSS; Stegmann et al. 1981
Hormonal Responses to Training
Acute exercise: cortisol + epinephrine spike (catabolic, mobilises fuel). Post-exercise: testosterone + IGF-1 + GH pulse (anabolic, rebuild). The crucial window: first 30 min post-run — protein + carbs maximise the anabolic response (Ivy 2004). Chronic overtraining: cortisol remains elevated, testosterone falls → chronic fatigue, performance plateau, immune suppression. Sleep: 80% of daily GH is released in stage 3–4 sleep. 6h sleep = ~40% less GH compared to 8h = slower adaptation.
Hackney 2006; Walker 2017; Ivy 2004
VO₂Max — Complete Science
VO₂Max = cardiac output × arteriovenous O₂ difference. It is limited by: (1) cardiac output (heart rate × stroke volume), (2) oxygen carrying capacity (haemoglobin, blood volume), (3) muscle oxygen extraction (capillary density, mitochondria). Training improves all three. Your VO₂Max of 47 ml/kg/min: the components are cardiac output ≈ 16–17 L/min and a-vO₂ diff ≈ 12–13 ml/100ml. Elite HM runners: VO₂Max 65–75. Sub-elite: 55–65. Recreational: 45–55. You're at the top of recreational — one strong training block from sub-elite.
Bassett & Howley 2000; Wagner 2000; Saltin & Calbet 2006
The Running Gait Cycle — Mechanical Analysis
The running gait cycle has two phases: stance (35–40% at easy pace, decreasing to 20% at sprint) and flight (60–65%). Ground contact time (GCT) is the most important mechanical variable — elite HM runners: 180–200ms GCT; recreational: 250–300ms. Every 10ms reduction in GCT at the same speed = ~1% improvement in running economy. GCT decreases with: higher cadence, greater reactive strength, plyometric training, and improved elasticity of calf-Achilles complex. Your tight calves compromise the Achilles' elastic energy return — the Alfredson protocol directly addresses this.
Dorn et al. 2012; Barnes & Kilding 2015; Heiderscheit et al. 2011
Elastic Energy — The Spring Model
The human leg behaves like a spring during running: storing elastic strain energy during loading and returning it during push-off. The Achilles tendon stores and returns ~35% of total energy cost per stride (Alexander 1991). This is the most energetically important structure in running. Tight, stiff calves reduce this elastic compliance → more muscular work required → higher O₂ cost per km → reduced running economy. The eccentric calf protocol (Alfredson) progressively loads the tendon to increase its elastic capacity. After 12 weeks, running economy improvements of 3–5% are measurable.
Alexander 1991; Ker et al. 1987; Lichtwark & Wilson 2008
Cadence Optimisation — The Research
Preferred cadence tends to be ~6% below optimal (Cavanagh & Williams 1982). Optimal cadence minimises the metabolic cost of running through: (1) reduced ground contact time, (2) shorter lever arm (less knee extension overshoot), (3) reduced vertical oscillation, (4) lower impact forces per step. Heiderscheit et al. 2011 showed a 5% step rate increase reduced: peak hip adduction moment 32%, peak hip flexor moment 34%, energy absorption at hip 20%. Target 170–180 spm. Use metronome app for first 4–6 weeks. Each 5% increase takes 6–8 weeks to neurologically consolidate.
Heiderscheit et al. 2011; Cavanagh & Williams 1982; de Ruiter et al. 2014
Overstriding — Why It Matters
Overstriding occurs when the foot lands ahead of the body's centre of mass. This creates a braking impulse opposing forward motion. Every braking impulse must be overcome by propulsive force — wasted energy = higher O₂ cost. Overstriding causes: tibial stress fractures (heel strike creates 3× tibial bending moment vs midfoot), patellofemoral pain, plantar fasciitis. Rohan's tight calves limit ankle dorsiflexion → compensated by overstriding. Primary fix: Alfredson calf protocol + cadence work (overstriding reduced by 50% with 5% cadence increase, Heiderscheit 2011).
Lieberman et al. 2010; Heiderscheit et al. 2011; Daoud et al. 2012
Periodization — The Complete Framework
Periodization is the systematic manipulation of training variables (volume, intensity, frequency) over time to maximise adaptation while minimising overtraining. Three main models: (1) Linear: volume ↓ progressively as intensity ↑. Classic for novice runners — your 14-week plan uses this. (2) Undulating: volume and intensity vary daily/weekly. More complex, suited for advanced athletes. (3) Block periodization (Issurin): concentrated blocks of single-quality training (3–4 weeks aerobic → 3–4 weeks threshold → 2 weeks race-specific). Best evidence for maintaining fitness gains. Your 14-week plan is a linear-undulating hybrid with a 3-week taper — evidence-based for first HM.
Issurin 2010; Bompa & Haff 2009; Stöggl & Sperlich 2014
Tapering Science — Getting Race-Ready
Tapering is the progressive reduction of training load before a race to allow full supercompensation. Optimal HM taper: 2–3 weeks, reduce VOLUME by 40–60%, MAINTAIN intensity. The critical mistake: reducing both volume AND intensity = detraining + loss of race-specific neuromuscular readiness. Mujika & Padilla 2003: a 2-week taper with 40–60% volume reduction and maintained intensity improves HM performance by 2–3% (3–4 minutes for a 2:10 runner). Mechanisms: glycogen supercompensation, haematological adaptations, neuromuscular recovery, psychological restoration. Do NOT add extra sessions in taper week.
Mujika & Padilla 2003; Bosquet et al. 2007; Pyne et al. 2009
Overreaching vs. Overtraining
Functional overreaching (FOR): short-term performance decrement that, after recovery, leads to supercompensation. This is INTENTIONAL and happens in peak training weeks. Signs: elevated resting HR, heavy legs, reduced motivation — all normal for 1–3 days. Non-functional overreaching (NFOR): FOR that extends beyond 2 weeks. Requires 4–8 weeks recovery. Signs: chronic fatigue, immune suppression, mood disturbance. Overtraining syndrome (OTS): months of NFOR. Rare — requires complete rest for weeks/months. The ACWR system detects FOR early: ACWR > 1.5 = entering FOR territory. The goal is to reach FOR → recover → supercompensate → repeat at higher level.
Meeusen et al. 2013 (European College consensus); Gabbett 2016
Long-Term Athlete Development (LTAD)
Your 14-week plan is phase one of a multi-year development pathway. Research shows: Year 1 of serious training = 10–20% VO₂Max improvement. Year 2 = 5–10%. Years 3–5 = 2–5%/year diminishing returns. Elite HM times require VO₂Max 65–70 — approximately 4–7 years of consistent training from VO₂Max 47. The fastest path: consistent aerobic base → threshold development → speed reserve → race-specific work. Current week 1 focus: lay the foundation. Running age (years of consistent training) is as important as chronological age. Your running age: 0.
Lloyd & Oliver 2012; Bompa & Haff 2009
Pre-Race Psychology
Race anxiety is optimal at moderate arousal (Yerkes-Dodson 1908 — the inverted U). Too calm = underperformance. Too anxious = choking. Strategies: (1) Process focus: concentrate on controllable elements (cadence, breathing, pacing) rather than outcome (time, placing). (2) Pre-performance routine: consistent warm-up, music, mantras. (3) Reappraisal: anxiety symptoms (elevated HR, butterflies) are identical to excitement — labelling them as "excitement" improves performance (Brooks 2014). (4) If-then planning: "If legs feel heavy at km 5, I will shorten stride and increase cadence." These scripts prevent real-time decision-making under stress.
Yerkes & Dodson 1908; Brooks 2014; Weinberg & Gould 2015
Mental Fatigue and Performance
Mental fatigue impairs endurance performance independently of physical fatigue. Marcora et al. (2009): 90 min of cognitively demanding work before exercise reduced time to exhaustion by 15.1% at the same perceived exertion. The mechanism: mental fatigue increases perceived effort (RPE) for a given workload — it does not change physiology, but changes your brain's readiness to tolerate discomfort. For race day: avoid mentally exhausting activities the 24–48h before. For training: if RPE is unusually high despite normal pace, consider whether work/life stress is a contributing factor.
Marcora et al. 2009; Van Cutsem et al. 2017
Self-Determination Theory in Training
Ryan & Deci (2000): intrinsic motivation (the joy of running, personal mastery) is more sustainable than extrinsic (time goals, comparison). Three psychological needs: Autonomy (you choose your goals), Competence (you see improvement), Relatedness (community). The training log fills all three: logging creates a sense of competence (visible progress), the goals are personally meaningful (Bangalore, Sep 27), and sharing creates relatedness. Research consistently shows intrinsically motivated athletes train more consistently over years. Your sub-2hr goal should be YOURS — not a comparison number. The science behind running should fuel wonder, not anxiety.
Ryan & Deci 2000; Hagger & Chatzisarantis 2007
Altitude Physiology — 6000m Preparation
At 6000m: barometric pressure ≈ 354 mmHg (vs 760 sea level). Arterial PO₂ ≈ 45 mmHg (vs 95). SpO₂ ≈ 70–75%. Haemoglobin saturation curve: at 6000m you're on the steep descending portion — small altitude increases cause large O₂ drops. VO₂Max effectively halves: your 47 becomes ~26. Acclimatisation takes 3–4 weeks at altitude but protective adaptations begin immediately: hyperventilation, increased cardiac output, EPO release → reticulocyte increase → haematocrit increase (4–6 weeks). For 6000m expeditions: spend 10–14 days above 4000m before attempting summit. Your training now (aerobic base) is the most important preparation for altitude — every aerobic percent built is preserved at altitude.
West et al. 2012; Bartsch & Saltin 2008; Roach 2001
Heat Adaptation for Bangalore
Bangalore Sep 27: 24–28°C, 70–85% humidity. Heat stress increases core temperature → increased blood flow to skin for cooling → reduced cardiac output available for muscles → higher HR at same pace. Pugh et al. 1969: marathon times 5–10% slower in 25°C vs 10°C. Lorenzo et al. (2010): 10 days of heat acclimation (exercising in hot conditions 60–90 min/session) improves: plasma volume (+500ml), sweat rate (+15%), threshold HR (lower), performance in heat AND cool conditions (cross-adaptation). Protocol: from Sep 13, do 20–30 min easy runs at mid-day (11am–2pm) 3× per week. Hydrate aggressively during this period.
Lorenzo et al. 2010; Pugh et al. 1969; Sawka et al. 2011

Half marathon is ~97% aerobic. Understanding your three energy systems directly dictates pacing, nutrition timing, and training zone distribution.
SYSTEM 01 · 0–10 SEC

ATP-PCr · Phosphagen

Zero oxygen. Stored ATP + phosphocreatine. Peak power ~2000W. Depletes in 10 sec. Not relevant for HM racing but used in strides and hill sprints during training — loading fast-twitch motor units that reduce ground contact time by 3–5% over a 12-week programme.
Cramer (2008): PCr resynthesis is 84% at 2 min, 98% at 8 min — why strength sets need full rest between.
SYSTEM 02 · 10 SEC – 2 MIN

Glycolytic · Anaerobic

No oxygen. Muscle glycogen → pyruvate → lactate. Fast ATP but accumulates H⁺ ions causing acidosis and fatigue. Contributes <3% during HM. But your lactate threshold — where aerobic capacity meets glycolytic rate — determines your sustainable race pace. Threshold training pushes this boundary up.
Brooks (2018): Lactate is not waste — it is a fuel, signaling molecule, and gluconeogenic precursor. Training at threshold trains faster lactate clearance.
SYSTEM 03 · 2 MIN – ∞

Oxidative · Aerobic

Requires oxygen. Fuel: glycogen + fat (ratio shifts with intensity). Zone 2: ~50% fat / 50% carb. HM race pace (Zone 4): ~90% carbs. You carry ~400g glycogen = ~1600 kcal. HM burns 1200–1400 kcal. Running on fumes at km 17 if under-fueled — hence gels at km 7 and km 14.
Romijn et al. (1993): Carbohydrate oxidation at 85% VO₂Max = 3.5 g/min. 400g glycogen ÷ 3.5 = ~114 min. Your race window is tight — fuel before depletion.

The 80/20 Rule — Seiler (2010)

Elite endurance athletes distribute training as ~80% below threshold (Zone 1–2) and only 20% at or above threshold (Zone 3–5). Going moderate all the time — "junk miles" — produces inferior adaptations to either approach. Every run must have a clear zone intention.
80% — The Base
Zone 1–2. Builds mitochondria, fat oxidation, capillary bed, cardiac output. Should feel almost too easy. Full conversation pace. This is where your aerobic engine is built.
20% — The Edge
Zone 4–5. Threshold runs, intervals, strides. Raises the ceiling. Improves lactate threshold and running economy. Hard to hold conversation. Never more than 20% of weekly volume.

Running economy (RE) — oxygen cost per km — is trainable. Elite runners use 5–10% less oxygen at the same speed. Cadence, posture, arm swing, and foot strike are the four pillars. You need consistent form, not perfect form.

👟 Cadence · The Magic Number

Target: 170–180 steps/min. Most recreational runners run 155–165. Every 5% cadence increase reduces impact force ~11% (Heiderscheit et al., 2011). Higher cadence = shorter ground contact = less braking = better economy.
How: Metronome app at 174 bpm. Increase 5% over 4 weeks — never jump directly. 6–8 weeks to neurologically rewire. Start Week 3 of this plan.

📐 Posture

Forward lean 5–10° from ankles (not waist). Imagine controlled falling forward — gravity assists propulsion. Hips forward. Head neutral, eyes 15 m ahead. Chin parallel to ground. Relaxed jaw.
Shoulder test: Can you roll shoulders mid-run? If not, you're holding tension. Shake hands loose every 15 min. Shoulders down and back, not hunched.

💪 Arm Swing

90° elbow angle. Swing elbows back (not hands forward). Hands at hip height on backswing. Arms and legs are linked — cadence follows arm tempo. Loose fists (hold a crisp without crushing it).
Don't: Cross the midline. Arms crossing wastes lateral energy and creates unhelpful rotation. Parallel to direction of travel only.

👣 Foot Strike

Land under your centre of mass, not out front. Overstriding (heel strike ahead of hip) is the #1 cause of braking force and tibial stress. Mid/forefoot preferred but position relative to hip is what matters.
Your calf tightness limits ankle dorsiflexion → direct cause of overstriding. The Alfredson calf protocol IS your foot strike fix. Do it daily.

💨 Breathing

2:2 pattern (inhale 2 steps, exhale 2 steps). At race pace: 2:1 or 3:2. Belly breathing activates diaphragm — your primary respiratory muscle. Chest breathing is inefficient and causes early fatigue.
Side stitch: Diaphragm cramp. Exhale on alternating foot strikes. Slow pace, press hand on stitch site, breathe deliberately.

📊 Running Economy

Elite RE: ~180–210 mL O₂/kg/km. Recreational: ~240–260. A 10% RE improvement effectively equals raising VO₂Max by 5 points without an actual VO₂Max change — it multiplies your existing engine.
RE builders: (1) 8×100m strides 2×/week from Wk 3 (2) Strength (squats, deadlifts) — Beattie 2017: 6–8% improvement (3) Plyometrics Wk 7+ (4) Threshold runs (5) Simply: more running at correct paces.

💊 Key Micronutrients

Iron: Foot-strike hemolysis → 10–15% higher iron need. Fatigue at easy paces = first sign of iron deficiency. Blood test every 6 months.
Vitamin D: Muscle function + bone remodeling. Most Indians deficient. 2000–4000 IU/day. Test baseline levels.
Magnesium: 300+ enzymatic reactions. Cramping = Mg + Na + K depletion, not just dehydration. 400 mg/day from food + supplement if needed.
Sodium: For runs >90 min. 500–1000 mg/hr. Water alone without electrolytes → hyponatremia (race-ending, potentially fatal).

☕ Caffeine — The Legal Performance Drug

Dose: 3–6 mg/kg = 234–468 mg for 78 kg. 3–4 cups of Bangalore filter coffee. Timing: 60 min before race start. Effect: reduces RPE 5–6%, improves time-to-exhaustion 12% (Pickering & Grgic, 2019). Regular coffee drinkers: maintain intake — don't stop pre-race (withdrawal headache risk). Filter coffee ~80–100 mg/cup: 3 cups = 240–300 mg. Effective dose. Never try caffeine gels on race day without practicing in training first — GI distress from novel products ends races.

😴 Sleep — The Foundation

Target: 8–9 hours. Deep sleep stages 3–4 = pulsatile growth hormone release — primary driver of tissue repair and muscle protein synthesis. Sleep-deprived athletes: 20–30% higher injury risk + 10–15% lower VO₂Max expression (Walker, 2017).
Protocol: Consistent wake time. Blackout curtains. 18–20°C room. No screens 45 min before bed. Last meal 3h pre-sleep (body temp must drop for sleep onset).
Mah et al. (2011, Stanford): Athletes given 10h sleep showed 9% sprint improvement + significantly reduced injury rate.

Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio = last 7 days ÷ last 28 days weekly average. Sweet spot: 0.8–1.3. Above 1.5 = danger zone (injury risk doubles). Below 0.8 = undertraining (Gabbett, 2016).

Rohan-Specific · Cascade Analysis

Your two injury chains

Injury rarely happens at the site of pain. It happens because a restriction elsewhere creates compensatory overload downstream. Both chains below are documented in your assessment and confirmed by your Jun 19 walking data.

These are not hypothetical. Your walking HR of 129 bpm at 10:16/km pace is consistent with the neuromuscular inefficiency pattern of hip flexor inhibition of glute max activation — Chain 1, step 3 is already active.

Chain 1: Tight hip flexors → 7-step cascade

1. Tight psoas + iliacus
Shortened resting length. Limited hip extension ROM. Normal running requires 10–15° hip extension. Tight hip flexors restrict this to 5–8°.
→ Your documented issue
2. Anterior pelvic tilt
Psoas pulls the anterior iliac spine downward, rotating pelvis forward → increased lumbar lordosis. Levine 1999: anterior tilt increases lumbar extension load by 40%. Over marathon distances = chronic back pain. At 6000m with 20kg pack this becomes dangerous.
3. Reciprocal inhibition of gluteus maximus
Neurologically, tight hip flexors suppress (inhibit) the opposing glute max. "Gluteal amnesia." Your most powerful running muscle is being turned off. Bullock-Saxton 1994: tight hip flexors predict 23% reduction in glute max activation.
4. Hamstring overload (compensation)
With glute max inhibited, hamstrings must compensate for ALL hip extension during push-off — a role they're not designed for. Result: hamstring fatigue → proximal hamstring tendinopathy → acute strain. Chumanov et al. 2012: hamstrings compensate 55% more when glute max is inhibited.
5. Knee valgus + patellar maltracking
Reduced glute med activation → lateral hip drop → femoral internal rotation → knee cave (valgus) → patella tracks laterally → patellofemoral pain syndrome. Taunton et al. 2002: 42% of running injuries are knee-related — most trace back to hip dysfunction.
6. Medial tibial stress syndrome
Knee valgus rotates the tibia internally. Over thousands of foot strikes, rotational stress on tibia → shin splints → at ultra mileage → tibial stress fracture. Risk accelerates at weeks 6–10 of a training programme without corrective work.
7. Lumbar spine degeneration (long-term)
For your ultra/mountaineering goals: carrying a 20kg pack with anterior pelvic tilt for 10+ hours compresses L1–L5. Over multiple expeditions = disc degeneration. Hip flexor flexibility + strength is the primary preventive factor.

Chain 2: Tight calves → 6-step cascade

1. Tight gastrocnemius + soleus
Limited ankle dorsiflexion ROM. Normal running requires 20–30° dorsiflexion at midstance. Tight calves restrict this to 10–15°. Your body must compensate to get your shin over your foot.
→ Your documented issue
2. Excessive pronation (foot collapse)
When the ankle cannot dorsiflex sufficiently, the foot compensates by rolling inward (pronating) excessively — the subtalar joint unlocks prematurely. Powers 2003: limited dorsiflexion correlates with 2.5× higher pronation velocity.
3. Plantar fascia overload
Excessive pronation places plantar fascia under eccentric tensile stress at push-off. Riddle et al. 2003: limited dorsiflexion is the #1 independent predictor of plantar fasciitis (OR 23.3). Morning heel pain is the first sign.
4. Achilles tendon strain
Tight gastro/soleus increases tensile load on Achilles with every foot strike. Result: Achilles tendinopathy — a degenerative condition that does NOT heal with rest alone. Requires eccentric loading (Alfredson 1998). Onset typically at weeks 4–8 of new training. Your prevention window is now.
5. Medial tibial stress syndrome (MTSS)
Tibialis posterior and anterior overload from compensating for limited dorsiflexion → periosteal stress on medial tibia. "Shin splints." Untreated: tibial stress reaction → stress fracture. Moen et al. 2009: limited dorsiflexion present in 72% of MTSS cases.
6. Lateral ankle instability
Limited dorsiflexion alters the forefoot's landing position, increasing ankle inversion risk. Peroneals are at mechanical disadvantage when the calf is tight and the ankle restricted. Critical for trail and mountaineering — one ankle sprain at altitude is an emergency.
Prevention: Both chains are addressed simultaneously. Hip flexor stretching alone (without glute activation) treats only step 1 of Chain 1. Calf stretching alone (without eccentric loading) treats only step 1 of Chain 2. The full protocol in the Exercises panel addresses every step of both chains.
